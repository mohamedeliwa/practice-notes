# Chest X-ray Interpretation

Steps for optimal chest X-ray

- Accurate patient positioning
- Tube - Film distance
- Full inspiration
- Adequate penetration

## Different views of chest X-ray:

PA (Postro - anterior) view

    - The most popular chest X-ray

AP (Antro - posterior) view

    - should expect some magnification in the size of the heart (as the heart is away from the film)

Erect vs Supine views

    -  in erect the patient is able to have full inspiration (can have accurate assessment of cardiac shadow)
    - in the supine position, the diaphragm is relatively high, the heart will be flattened (will appear as if it's enlarged)
    -

Lateral view

    - if the cardiac size is questionable, we should have the lateral view, as there is no magnification of the heart in the lateral view
    - we should decide the side of the lateral view, if the pathology in the left lung , we should have left lateral view, and vice versa

Lordotic view

    - obtained in the AP projecting with the patient tilted about 45 degress relative to the x-ray beam.
    - one of its values is to displace the shadow of the clavecle away from the area of the lung apex
    - one of its values is to augment the appearance of middle lobe lesions

## Diaphram Level

- the right copula is relatively higher than the left copula by 1-3 cm

## Pleural Effusion

- obliteration of the costo-phernic angles, either in the PA or lateral views
- to confirm it, put the patient on the side and have an x-ray, you will see the fuild moves upward, or have a chest US scan

-

## Full inspiration VS Copula level

- level of copula at the level of 10th posterior rib
- level of copula at the level of 6th anterior rib

## Lateral view vs Dorsal Vertebrae

- in the lateral view we should notice that normally the upper dorsal vertebrae are white while the lower dorsal vertebrae are black
- because the upper vertebrae are superimposed by the shoulder shadow
- while the lower vertebrae are super-imposed by the lung bases containing air
- if the lateral view shows no difference between the upper and lower vertebrae,
  (the lower are white) the we suspect a lesion in the base of the lung

## Normal PA film without rotation

- the spinous process of doral vertebrae should be in the middle between the two clavicles
- if the patient is rotated , you might get one of the lungs is hypertranslucent and the other lungs is veiled (bcz one of lung is closer to the film than the opposite one)
-

## PA view vs Lung zone

- lung apex
- upper zone
- mid zone (at the level of hilum)
- lower zone

## lateral view vs Lung lobes

- on left side there is a major fissure
- on right side there is a major and a minor fissures

## Silhouette sign

- there is an opacity in the lung has masked the cardiac border, meaning the lesion is located anteriorly
- the anteriorly located region in RT lung is the middle lobe

## Air bronchogram

- there is an opacity in the lung through which are multiple linear air translucences, representing air filled bronchi
- if there is air bronchogram, this means the lesion is of pulmonary origin, and it is of alveolar pathology (all of this means consolidation)
- pneumonia is one of the causes of consolidation

## Pulmonary nodule

- well defined lesion which is equal or less than 3 cm in diameter

## Pulmonary mass

- well defined lesion which is more than 3 cm in diameter

## Pulmonary patch

- ill defined lesion containing air bronchogram

## Pulmonary cavity

- well defined lesion containing air (totally or partially)

## How to interpret the chest X-ray

- ensure optimal quality radiograph
- patient data and previous films
- discriminate betwee Focal and Diffuse pathologies
- CT support

### Focal pathology

by focal we mean we have 1, 2, 3.. or more lesions but the rest of the lugs are free
by diffuse we mean the whole lung field is involved by the disease

Focal lesion possibilities:

- nodules
- masses
- patches
- cavities

### Nodules

- most common DD:

  - Tuberculoma
  - Hamartoma
  - Bronchogenic ca.
  - Metastases "most common"
  - AVM
  - Hydatid cyst

- if there are multiple nodules in the lung consider metastases specially if you don't have the clinical data of the patient
